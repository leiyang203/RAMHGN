Abstract
Background Drug repositioning offers a promising avenue for accelerating drug development and reducing costs.
Recently, computational repositioning approaches have gained attraction for identifying potential drug-disease
associations (DDAs). Biological entities such as drugs, genes, proteins, RNA, and diseases interact within a complex
network. How to adequately extract the intrinsic relationships among them and accurately predict the drug-disease
associations remains a challenge.
Methods In this study, we introduce MRDDA, a novel graph neural network model that utilizes multiple relations,
for drug repositioning. First, we design a hybrid graph convolutional framework to capture both local and global
representations of drugs and diseases. Subsequently, a meta-path-based approach is employed to capture high-order
topological representations from these entities. Finally, we present a layer-wise attention mechanism to integrate
embeddings from various layers.
Results The MRDDA model demonstrated superior performance in predicting drug-disease associations compared
to existing methods, achieving higher results during the 10-fold cross-validation on three benchmark datasets.
Notably, in our case studies focusing on Alzheimerтs disease and breast cancer, MRDDA effectively identified several
promising drug candidates that were previously unrecognized for these conditions. Additionally, molecular docking experiments reinforced our results by confirming the binding affinities and interactions between selected drugs
and their target diseases, suggesting a solid basis for further experimental studies.
Conclusion MRDDA offers an innovative framework for drug repositioning by effectively modeling and predicting
drug-disease associations through advanced graph neural network techniques. The modelтs ability to integrate multirelational data with greater accuracy paves the way for more efficient identification of potential therapeutic uses
for existing drugs, ultimately contributing to the acceleration of drug development and reduced costs in the pharmaceutical industry.

Introduction
Drug discovery, a cornerstone of pharmaceutical
research, is important in the development of new treatments for various diseases. Traditional drug discovery is
marked by extensive timelines, elevated costs, and significant attrition [1]. This has spurred growing interest in
alternative strategies to identify new treatment options.
Rapid advances in artificial intelligence have boosted
interest in drug repositioning for cost-effective medical
discoveries. Numerous applications highlight the essential role of computational drug repositioning in revolutionizing pharmaceutical development. [2т6]
Computational strategies for drug repositioning are
categorized into three primary types: network-based
approaches, machine learning techniques, and deep
learning frameworks. Specifically, network-based
approaches predict unknown drug-disease associations
(DDAs) through the synthesis and examination of biological network architectures. For example, Luo et al.
[7] introduced MBiRW, which establishes a drug-disease heterogeneous network via similarity metrics and
utilizes a Bi-Random Walk algorithm to discover new
applications for existing drugs. Likewise, Chen et al. [8]
created ProbS and HeatS, forecasting DDAs by harnessing essential network topology metrics. However, the
success of these methods often depends on the integrity
and thoroughness of the available network data, with
partial or incorrect data potentially resulting in unreliable predictions.
Over the past decades, researchers have explored
machine learning algorithms for drug repositioning.
These approaches utilize large-scale biological and
chemical data to search new therapeutic uses for existing drugs. Gottlieb et al. [9] proposed PREDICT for
large-scale drug indication prediction. This method uses
multiple similarity measures to infer potential DDAs.
PREDICT quantifies similarities between drug-disease
pairs and ranks the accumulated evidence using logistic regression. Zhou et al. [10] introduced NEDD, which
employs meta-path-based method on a heterogeneous
drug-disease graph to derive embeddings. A classifier is
then trained to identify potential DDAs. Yang et al.[11]
proposed MKDGRLS for drug repositioning. They calculated multiple kernels for drugs and diseases, integrated
them with Laplacian regularization in MKDGRLS, and
used the Alternating Least Squares algorithm for prediction. Zhao et al.[12] introduced DDAGDL, which utilizes
an attention-based geometric deep learning network to
learn feature representations, ultimately employing the
use of XGBoost for the classification task. Zhang et al.
[13] propose NCH-DDA, which employs single- and
multi-neighborhood modules along with contrastive
learning to learn the information of drugs and diseases.

Recently, the surge in deep learning has sparked
numerous research endeavors. Deep learning maps
high-dimensional sparse features to lower-dimensional
spaces, preserving as much information as possible.
Graph neural networks (GNNs), a prominent branch of
deep learning, have shown notable advancements in drug
repositioning tasks. For instance, Yu et al. [14] introduced LAGCN, utilizing graph convolution to generate
embeddings for drugs and diseases and integrating them
through an attention mechanism. Tang et al. [15] further
expanded this area with DRGBCN, which merges varied data sources to predict potential DDAs. This model
uses graph convolution paired with a layer attention
mechanism to derive embeddings, supplemented by a
bilinear attention network that delineates the relationships of DDA pairs. Sun et al. [16] architected PSGCN,
which extracts context-specific information from partner graphs and employs layer attention to assimilate
features across multiple scales. To capture fine-grained,
context-specific information within network structures,
subgraph-based methods have emerged as a complementary approach to GNNs. For example, Wang et al.
[17] propose HGDDA, a hypergraph learning method
with subgraph matching to predict DDAs. It derives
H-hop subgraphs from a bipartite drugтdisease association graph and then employs hypergraph convolution to
extract subgraph features for accurate association prediction. Sun et al. [18] propose SAGCN, which builds a heterogeneous network and uses a GCN-equipped subgraph
extractor to capture 1- and 2-hop features, integrating
them through attention mechanisms to learn node representations for prediction. Shang et al. [19] propose HNFDDA, a transformer-based model for DDA prediction. It
derives subgraphs based on first-order common neighbors and then applies subgraph contrastive learning to
extract node information.
Despite the promising outcomes achieved by the aforementioned methods in drug repositioning, they exhibit
notable limitations. Most existing methods primarily
focus on extracting features from complex drug-disease
networks, often neglecting the broader biological context. While some methods employ subgraphs to capture
local topological information, these are typically limited
to extracting specific association patterns rather than
modeling comprehensive relational structures. In reality,
biological data encompass diverse entities and interactions, presenting two main challenges. The first challenge
is to integrate various types of biological data while preserving their unique details. The second challenge is to
capture both high-order topological associations and
interconnections between entities.
To address these challenges, we propose a novel
method called MRDDA, which distinguishes itself by

Experimental setting

In our studies, we implemented 10-fold cross-validation (10-CV) to assess the performance of the proposed
model. Each fold consisted of using a single subset for
testing data while utilizing the remaining nine subsets for
training. We removed drug-disease relationship structures that appear in the test set when constructing the
heterogeneous network for training. The effectiveness of
MRDDA across three benchmark datasets was assessed
by utilizing the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) and the area under the precisionrecall curve (AUPR) as evaluation measures.

Declarations

Conclusion
In this research, we present MRDDA, an innovative
computational method designed to identify drug-disease associations (DDAs). By integrating information
from various biological datasets, we create a heterogeneous network specific to DDAs. MRDDA utilizes a
hybrid graph convolution framework to capture both
broad and detailed features of drugs and diseases. It
also employs a meta-path-based approach to uncover
complex topological patterns in the network. Additionally, an attention mechanism is used to merge embeddings from different layers effectively. Experimental
results demonstrate MRDDAтs superiority over existing
prediction models. Comprehensive case studies further
highlight its potential as a powerful tool for discovering new drug-disease connections, especially valuable
for newly developed drugs that lack established associations. Despite its proven effectiveness, MRDDA has
several limitations. One key limitation is the insufficient
exploitation of diverse similarity measures between
drugs and diseases, which may lead to an under-utilization of available information. Enhancing the model by
integrating richer similarity descriptors could improve
its predictive accuracy. Furthermore, the scalability of
MRDDA to larger, more complex biological networks
deserves further exploration. In future work, we plan to
address these limitations by incorporating multifaceted
similarity metrics, optimizing the model for large-scale
biological datasets, and exploring transfer learning
techniques to adapt MRDDA to other drug discovery
tasks. These efforts aim to further enhance the utility
and generalizability of MRDDA in computational drug
repositioning.
